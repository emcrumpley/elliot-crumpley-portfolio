Overview

An analytics product that spoke Expedia's language, not the partner's

Expedia Group's Analytics section in Partner Central gave hotel partners access to performance data — but the experience had accumulated years of internal terminology, fragmented navigation, and data without context. Partners couldn't find what they needed, didn't trust what they saw, and were spending hours manually cross-checking Expedia's numbers against their own sources.

Terms like "Competitiveness," "Production," "Net Room Nights," and "Offer strength" made sense to Expedia's product teams but were opaque to the hotel managers and revenue teams who depended on the tool daily. The navigation mirrored Expedia's internal product structure — not how partners actually thought about their business.

In early 2020, the team undertook a full redesign. I led the content design effort: auditing the existing experience, rebuilding the IA from research findings, defining data visualization principles, and creating a content system scalable enough to extend across all of Partner Central.

Research foundation

What 18 partner interviews revealed

A generative research study with 18 partners across EMEA, AMER, and APAC — spanning A, B, and C segments — revealed three distinct modes of analytics use. Critically, segment didn't predict behavior: engagement and ambition did. This reframed how we thought about content and IA.

Monitoring Daily

Partners taking a quick pulse check on their property — visibility, conversions, guests. They want to capture performance at a glance without having to navigate deep. Entry points: Property analytics, Quality score.

Deep dive Occasional

Partners investigating pricing and visibility strategy, comparing against competitors and the same period last year. They play with filters to answer specific questions. Entry points: Visibility, Conversion.

Reporting Periodic

Partners exporting data to share with staff or cross-reference with other sources before major reporting periods. They need a customisable, trustworthy export they can actually use in business meetings.

Analytics section is vital for me, it tells me what's wrong and then the marketing section offers me the opportunity to drive actions to help the business to capture more occupancy... Even if we're a small property we can compete effectively.

— Partner interview participant (Hoss)

Key insight from research: Partners qualified Expedia's information as "fragmented" and wouldn't take action based on it alone. They were manually double-checking data in spreadsheets and doing their own market analysis on Google — not because they lacked sophistication, but because they didn't trust what we showed them.

Content audit

Auditing 50+ screens for friction points

Before redesigning anything, I conducted a systematic content audit across the entire Analytics space — documenting every label, heading, tooltip, and CTA that created confusion or inconsistency.

The audit uncovered patterns of failure that went beyond individual copy issues: inconsistent capitalisation, internal abbreviations presented as labels, contradictory visual signals (a red upward arrow), and metrics named after Expedia's data models rather than partner business concepts.

Before — internal language

Offer strength — partners didn't know what was being measured
Production — an internal term with no hotel-industry equivalent
Competitiveness — vague; partners asked "competitive compared to what?"
Net Room Nights — not how hotel managers track occupancy
Compset ADR — unexpanded abbreviation unfamiliar to smaller partners
Hotel ADR — should be "Average daily rate (ADR)" on first use

After — partner language

Partner strength — renamed to reflect the partner's standing
Your visibility — reframed around what the partner owns and controls
How you compare — contextualised against competitive set
Room nights booked — matches how revenue managers speak
Competitive set average daily rate — spelled out on first use
Sentence case throughout — applied consistently to all labels and nav items

Audit also identified: missing periods on instructional copy, inconsistent filter labelling, redundant navigation items, and CTAs with mismatched button/label copy.

Information architecture

Rebuilding navigation around partner mental models

The existing Analytics navigation had 12+ L3 items using Expedia-specific terminology. Partners struggled to predict what they'd find behind each label — and the structure didn't map to any of the three use cases the research revealed. I redesigned the IA into four buckets that matched how partners actually think about their business.

Old navigation New navigation Rationale
Property analytics, Offer strength, Quality score, Sort order Your visibility Grouped by the question partners actually ask: "How visible is my property right now and why?"
Guest value, Booking insights, Guest insights, Guest experience Traveler insights Bundled guest data so partners get a complete picture of who books and why — with one-click promotion creation surfaced in context
Visibility, Conversion (separate, siloed) Conversions Reframed as the impact of all actions taken — not just a funnel metric — linking partner behaviour to outcomes
Partner reporting suite, Offer strength (export) Reports A dedicated, customisable export hub — acknowledging that reporting is a distinct job-to-be-done, not an afterthought

IA principle: Instead of asking partners to learn Expedia's taxonomy, we mapped the navigation to the questions partners already arrive with: "How am I performing?", "Who are my guests?", "Are my actions working?", "What can I export?"

Scalable content system

Data visualization principles for the whole platform

One of the most consequential contributions was moving beyond fixing individual screens to establishing principles that could govern how data was communicated across all of Partner Central — not just Analytics.

The core question: when should we use a number vs a bar chart vs a line graph? And how should labelling work consistently regardless of which part of the platform the partner is in?

Right chart for the right context

Defined rules for when to use line graphs (trends over time), bar charts (comparisons at a point in time), and single-number callouts (immediate status metrics). Removed ambiguity for engineers and designers adding new modules.

Labelling that adds context

Every data point needed a label that communicated what it means, not just what it is. Numbers without context create anxiety — numbers with a baseline or trend create confidence.

Actionability over information

Principle: don't surface a metric without surfacing what the partner can do about it. Metrics linked to recommended actions where possible; passive data display was explicitly deprioritised.

Granularity by user need

Monitoring partners need high-level snapshots; deep-dive partners need filter controls and historical comparisons. The content system defined which level of detail belonged at which entry point.

Process

From swarm to strategy

The project ran over several months and moved through four distinct phases, with content design embedded at every stage rather than brought in at the end.

01
Generative research
Synthesised findings from 18 partner interviews to identify the three use cases (monitoring, deep dive, reporting) and the trust deficit at the heart of the experience.
02
Content audit
Audited 50+ screens for terminology, capitalisation, labelling consistency, and IA problems. Documented every friction point with a specific recommendation.
03
IA & vision
Rebuilt the navigation structure around partner mental models, defined the four content buckets, and created the "Analytics as a Trusted Friend" north-star vision.
04
Scalable system
Codified data viz principles and content rules that could extend beyond Analytics to all of Partner Central — designed to survive any single product pivot.

Vision

Analytics as a "Trusted Friend"

The north-star vision I articulated for the project: make Analytics feel like a trusted friend who knows your property, understands your market, and surfaces the right information at the right moment — without requiring the partner to know what to ask for.

This framing shaped every content decision: from the tone of recommendation copy ("Here's what we're seeing in your market" rather than "Your performance is below average") to the structure of the dashboard (leading with actionable insights, not raw data tables).

Give me a better story about my market thanks to the whys behind the displayed figures.

— Partner interview participant (Fernando) — the brief in a single sentence

Ideal partner experience — three dimensions

Think

I can quickly find data relevant to my property. I'm shown metrics affecting my revenue without having to dig for them. Expedia uses its data to help me generate more revenue through actionable items.

Feel

I feel confident in the accuracy of what I'm shown. Expedia has my best interest in mind. My property has a real opportunity to improve through the insights I receive.

Do

Easily export relevant data for my reporting needs. Take actions with the biggest impact on my property. Adjust rates, content, and availability with confidence that the data is accurate.

Reflection

What I learned

This project taught me that a content audit is as much a strategic document as a tactical one. Cataloguing what's broken isn't the point — the point is identifying the pattern behind the breaks, so you can fix the system rather than the symptoms.

I also learned the value of research as a content brief. The three partner modes — monitoring, deep dive, reporting — gave me a framework for every IA and copy decision for the rest of the project. When you're debating whether a metric belongs on an overview page or deeper in the hierarchy, "what mode is the partner in right now?" resolves it fast.

And building for scale meant accepting that some of the most important work would be invisible — the principles that prevented bad content decisions being made downstream, long after I'd moved on to other things.
